      The Conservative–Republican Party (CON–REP) is a political alliance of centrist to right-wing political parties. The alliance is dominated by the Conservative Party, Republican Party, and the Liberal Party. The alliance also includes smaller non-elected parties such as the Alliance Party and the Libetarian Party, and various other smaller state or local level political party and organizations. The alliance is currently in the opposition in Congress with a minority in the House of Representatives and in the Senate. The alliance is currently the second largest political entity in the United States after the Labour Party, who are the primary opponents of the alliance. The alliance last held a majority in the U.S. Senate from the 2055 to 2057, and a majority in the House of Representatives from 2050 to 2052. The top position in the alliance is the Chair which alternates between being held by a member of the Conservatives and Republicans every term, with the second highest position of the Deputy Chair going to the other party.

      The CON–REP alliance was formed on December 18th 2010, intitally as a simple elctoral alliance, with it becoming a more formalized cooperative coalition after the Mutual Election Competitiveness Agreement was codified on May 27th, 2018. The two parties began by discussing a mutual platform agreement and to not run opposing candidates in competitive districts. In the 2010 midterm elections, talks were amplified due to the 2010 Midterm elections which saw the Republican Party and Conservatives both lost a significant number of seats, and Republicans lost their majority in both chambers for the first time since 1994. Due to his loss, and the rising Labour Party, both parties agreed to an electoral alliance codified in the Mutual Cooperation Agreement of 2010. After the 2016 Presidential election which saw a right-wing candidate lose the Presidency for the first time since 2001, a second agreement was decided upon, which modified the agreement from that of a an electoral alliance to that of a political alliance.

      The alliance has held a majority in the U.S. House of Representatives from 2012 to 2016, 2020 to 2044, and from 2050 until 2056. In the Senate, the alliance has held a majority in the Senate from 2018 until 2046. They have also held a supermajority in the House from 2050 until 2056. Since the establishment of the alliance there have been two Presidents elected. The last President who was from the alliance was Jacob Villarreal from the Conservative Party, and the son of 41st President of the United States Trent Villarreal. The Chair of the alliance is elected directly by members of the party and serves for a 4 year renewable term. The current Chair of the alliance is Helena Elizondo, a former member of the executive board of the United Tennis Federation, who is from the Conservative Party. The incumbent Deputy Chair is Republican Julius Randall, who served a member of the U.S. Senate from 2004 until 2038. The Conservatives and Republican parties each maintain a degree of autonomy, with both parties having their own Congressional caucuses as well as organizational wings. However, the two parties do jointly own the American Institute on Democracy which is a Conservative think tank. The McHale and Villarreal families are the two most influential families in the party. The CON–REP origins of the alliance can be traced to multiple organizations such as the short-lived National Conservative Pact (1934–1940), the Conservative Alliance USA, and the Anti-Communist League, which was by far the most successful and influential organization prior to the CON–REP alliance.

      The alliance can be considered broadly centre-right in its poltiical positions, however there is a large variation in beliefs held by members of the alliance. The majority of the alliance's core supporters are pro-second amendment, pro-life, are against a strong social safety net, against Universal Healthcare, are in favor of lowering taxes for the rich, for resitricting illegal immigration in to the United States, and are in favor of deregulation. The primary demographic characteristics for supporters of the alliance are non-college educated men, living in rural areas. With additionally strong support from Evangelicals due in large part to the Conservative Party's strict anti-abortion and gay marriage views. As of January 20, 2056, the alliance currently holds 48 seats in the Senate (25 Conservative, 20 Republican, 3 Liberal), and 194 seats in the House of Representatives (70 Conservative, 56 Republican, 46 Liberal, 12 Alliance Party, 4 American Freedom Party, 4 People's party 1 for the Libertarian Party and 1 CON–REP allied independent). Since 2012, there have been four Presidents from the an alliance party. The alliance currently holds 50 out of 112 seats in the House of Delegates, with two of those seats being for the presiding officers of President of the House of Delegates and the Speaker of the House of Delegates, who are technically non-partisan.

      History

      2010–2018: Electoral Alliance

      Main article: 2010 United States Elections, 2012 United States Elections and 2016 United States Elections

      The alliance between the Conservative Party and the Republican Party began in 2010. The first compact between the two parties was agreed upon by the two parties in December of 2010, however discussions between the two parties had been occurring regularly since the Conservative Party was first able to disrupt the Republican Party in the 1976 Presidential elction and the Moral Revolution in the 1978 Midterm elections. The inital agreement between the two parties was primarily to avoid fielding candidates in the same electoral races in order to prevent a split vote on the right. The agreement was drawn up by former President Trent Villarreal, who hoped that the new alliance would foster unity amongst Conservatives and thus prevent parties such as the Democratic party from being able to gain control. Initially, the plans for cooperation between the two parties was rejected any idea of cooperation due to a history of competition between the two parties in various elections across the U.S. since the 1970s, as well as major ideological differences between the two parties. The two parties had been reluctant partners in Congress due to their shared Conservative values and their distain for the Democrats.

      In the 2010 midterms, there were 10 Congressional seats which were held by either the Conservatives or by the Republicans that ended up in split votes and a Democrat being able to claim victory. One of the seats included was held by Senate Majority Leader Evan O'Connor of Texas, resulting in the Republicans losing their majority in the House and Senate in the 2010 elections. After this shocking loss in the midterms, the Chair of the Republican National Committee and the Chair of the National Conservative Committee met on the same day in order to discuss futher strategy for the 2012 elections. Out of these negotiations, Emmanuel McHale announced on January 1st, 2011 that the two parties would be cooperating in the appointment of future judicial appointments. Then only two week later, a full comprehensive agreement was created and was passed by the two respective committees establishing an informal electoral alliance. In addition, the Assembly for the National Unity of the Moral Majority was created in April of 2011, in order to facilitate discussion between the two parties.

      By 2011, the agreement was officially adopted by all chapters of both parties. And in May of 2011, a shared policy platform was created ahead of the upcoming 2012 elections, known as the 2012 Manifesto for American Prosperity and Opporitunity. During this period, the ideology of the alliance was firmly in the centre-right on the political spectrum. The two parties, while differing on a handful of issues and with the Conservative Party being slightly to the right of the Republicans, still had a large amount of commonality in its beliefs. The early years of the alliance also saw the departures of many more hard-right figures from the party as the leadership of the party and of the alliance attempted to change the view of the Conservative Party away from that of a far-right party. This was also done in part because of the Republicans trying to moderate their allies views in order to uphold the reputation of the party as one of moderation. Notable departures from the Conservative Party included Butch Davidson, a far-right conspiracy theorist and anti-vaxxer, who was kicked from the party in August of 2012. Angelina Watkins, a right-wing YouTube star, was also kicked from the party in August of that year following accusations of racism and anti-semitism. However, not everyone was on board with the drastic shift to the center in the Conservative Party. Maya Delphine was one such person, she would continue to fight for her beliefs and spent a year from 2013 until 2014 trying to maintain the populist positions of the Conservative Party. By September of 2014, Delphine had given up and left the party in a public statement on her YouTube channel. She would then start a new political party called the American Freedom Party, which was named after the far-right political party started by former Maryland Governor and football coach Emmanuel McHale Sr.

      2012 was the first election in which the two parties were offically cooperating as a single alliance. Unfortunately for the alliance, the two parties continued to lose ground in the House of Representatives and the Senate. The alliance originally held 55 seats in the Senate, however their majority was reduced to 51, and their 236 seat majority in the House of Representatives was reduced to only 224 seats, just four seats shy of losing their majority in the House. This major loss in the election was made less severe due to the re-election of David Song to his second term as President. The alliance would also gain ground in local government winning in the Virginia Gubernatorial election. The alliance would also gain majorities in both chambers of the Virginia legislature, the Arizona legislature, the Michigan legislature, and the New Hampshire legislature. The election also saw the alliance win a seat in the state legislature of Hawaii for the first time. In 2014, the alliance parties would continue to gain ground of a fractured left-wing electorate. The rise of the Labour Party saw many of the left-wing vote being split between them and the Democratic party. This allowed for the alliance parties to maintain their majority in the House, and re-gain ground in the Senate. The two alliance parties together held 46 seats, while the Democrats and Labour held 36 and 14 seats respectively, with 2 seats held by the Justice Party. A coalition government was quickly formed by the two left-wing parties, leaving the alliance in the minority once again though. Alliance candidate David Maher won in the California gubernatorial election, which was the first time that a member of a conservative held the Governorship of California since 1976. The alliance also were able to grow their majority in the Florida legislature. Overall, the election was a success for the alliance parties, however they were not able to gain seats in the U.S. House of Representatives.

      The 2014 midterms saw the alliance lose their majority in the Senate for the first time in the alliance's history. The alliance parties would be entering minority government for the first time since 2004. However, they were able to grow their sizeable lead in the House of Representatives. CON–REP candidate Malcolm Glandville was able to win a second term as Governor of Texas. Peter Sevilla won in the New Mexico gubernatorial election, unseating incumbent Patricia Hernandez. Conservative Jeff Warren won in the Massachusettes gubernatorial election, defeating Democrat Shane Steele. The alliance also were able to win safe Conservative races such as the gubernatorial races in Alaska, Arkansas, Florida, Georgia, Kansas, and South Carolina

      The next election in 2016 was a major defeat for the alliance parties. The alliance held combined primaries for the first time, which included a total of 28 candidates. These included from the Conservative Party: Senator from Utah Sarah Maslow, former South Carolina state senator Raphael Edison, former Deputy Secretary of Education under Trent Villarreal Tina Everson, Governor of Arizona Mitchell Gladwell, and right-wing evangelical Senator Adam McHale from Missouri. Notable Republican candidates included: former Secretary of Defense Jason Pierre Turner, former Mayor of Washington D.C. Brian Goldson, Representative for West Virginia's 28th District Kendrick Osborne, anti-vaxxer Ashley Silverman, former U.S. Ambassador to NATO Ronald Wexler, and eventual Republican candidate, former Secretary of State under Trent Villarreal Michael Iversoft. Per the agreement ratified by the two cooperating parties, the Vice Presidential candiate must be a member of the Conservative Party. Thus, Mitchell Gladwell was selected as the Vice Presidential candidate for the Conservative-Republican ticket. However, the Iverson-Gladwell ticket was defeated in the general election to Democrats Eric Juarez and Justin Houston. Thus marking the first loss in the Presidential election in the history of the alliance, and the first time a Democrat had won the Presidency since Adam Kensington in 1996. Though the alliance were able to secure victories in multiple state legislative and gubernatorial elections.

      2018: A Turning Point

      The 2018 midterm elections was a massive success for the alliance parties. The alliance was able to re-take control of the Senate and the House of Representatives. In addition, the alliance were able to take control of multiple state legislatures and governorships. David Wilson was able to win re-election as the Senate Majority Leader, and Sean Casey of the Conservative Party from South Carolina's 3rd Congressional District was able to win election as the Speaker of the House of Representatives. In his first speech as the Speaker of the House, Casey promised to reverse the "crazy left-wing influence pervasive in the government". The Conservative Party became the largest party in the House for the first time in the history of the alliance. The Conservative Party would then influence the party to change the party's platofrm to take a harder position on immigration and on social issues such as abortion. However, the alliance was not able to win a majority in the Senate, losing 3 seats from 2016. Moderate Conservative Jerry Mosby was able to secure a fifth term as Senate Majority Leader, and Ezekiel Santana was re-elected as Majority Whip.

      The alliance also made large inroads in gaining back support from suburban voters, which had been was a critical demographic to election success. In particular, the alliance did really well in the suburban and affluent neighborhoods of Northern Virginia. This was interpreted by many as a rejection of what was believed to be radical left-wing policies being enacted by the Democratic establishment and by the rise of the Labour Party. Many would also see this victory as a sign that the moderate position was the best route in order to gain and maintain power in all levels of government. The alliance would also win in gubernatorial elections. Candidate Mark Foster became the first Republican Governor of Maryland since 2004, and the two parties were able to maintain control of state government in much of rural America.

      2018–2024: Early Years

      In 2018, the CON–REP alliance was re-organized from a simple electoral alliance into a full blown political alliance. This came after a series of meetings between Frank Morrison, the then leader of the Conservative Party and Zachary Edwards who was the Chair of the Republican National Committeee. After three months of discussion a draft outline known as the Articles of Liberty were published and agreed upon by the two parties. Following another five months of negotations, the Mutual Electoral Competitiveness Agreement was signed by both parties thus forming an official political alliance between the two parties. On August 2nd, 2019, the alliance was officially recognized by the Federal Election Commission as a legal political entity.

      2020: The First Test

      In 2020, the alliance would be put to the test for the first time with mixed results. The combined forces of the Conservative and Republican parties was not strong enough to surge the alliance into the White House, or gain a majority in either chamber of Congress, though the alliance did gain 9 seats in the House of Representatives. CON–REP candidate Jeffrey Solomon was able to unseat Democrat Roy Hodges in the North Carolina gubernatorial election. The alliance was also able to retain executive control over Utah, Montana, North Dakota, Missouri, Indiana, West Virginia, and New Hampshire. The alliance also made a significant impact in state elections, winning a majority in all of the states which voted for one of the alliance parties in 2016. They were also able to flip the Pennsylvania Attorney General from the Democratic Party to alliance candidate Selma Abudllah.

      2020 United States Presidential Election

      Jacob Villarreal lost the 2020 United States Presidential Election to incumbent Eric Juarez. Villarreal lost in key rust belt states including Arizona, Minnesota, Michigan, and Wisconsin. However, Villarreal was able to retain the key swing states of Georgia, Florida, North Carolina, Ohio and Texas. The CON–REP alliance was unable to win New Hampshire and Pennsylvania, which were won by Juarez by an average of 28,000 votes. Villarreal's camapign lost key swing states likely in part due to his reluctance to accept human-caused climate change. Furthermore, the loss came during a period of high approval for President Villarreal and when the public was generally content with the direction that the Democratic leadership in government was taking the country. The Conservative–Republican Party did well with its primary demographics of evangelicals and white non-college educated people under 35. The alliance saw record turnout in their favor from evangelicals who united under Villarreal who himself is a member of the evangelical United Church of Salvation. The 2020 election also saw the further use of social media marketing campaigns increase, with many campaigns utilizing social media marketing in order to drive out supporters to the polls. This had a postiive effect for both Democrats and CON–REP alliance candidates, however there was a stronger effect in favor of Democrats.

      A few notable Conservative and Republican politicans began their political carers in 2020. The most notable of which was the win of Helena Elizondo in Florida's 18th Congressional District. Elizondo's campaign was considered a grassroots and populist campaign in which she used her outsider status in order to rise in the polls. Elizondo was not considered likely to win the primary due to the long-standing Republican hold over the district. This would prove to not be the case as Elizondo would win convincingly in the primary election, and automatically won her seat due to no Democrat challenging the seat. Another notable figure who took office is Ash Blumenthal who was elected to the U.S. Senate for Texas. Doug Martindale would hold elected office for the first time, when he won the final seat required for the alliance to gain a majority in North Carolina's 2nd district. Many candidates running under the newly formed alliance ran as cultural conservative and fiscal moderates, with 32% categorizing themselves as "evangelical" or "very religious." The 2020 election saw the beginnings of a shift in policy from the traditionally centre-right policies into a more hard right-wing policy platform; though most of this change did not occur until decades later. Online media became the most dominant form of political dicourse for conservatives in the 2020 election, overtaking Fox News and other traditional outlets for the first time. Many who joined these online media platforms were those who believed that Fox News was not as right-wing it had been in the past; while other believed that the mainstream media outlets were not moderate enough.

      For a short time, this division between old media and new created a rift in the American right. Some more hardliners believed that the party should become more invested in the culture wars, while others believed sticking with the traditionally conservative economic and social principles was the best course of action. This temporary rift lead to some believing that alliance should end due to perceived ideological schisms between the majoritty moderate centre-right Republicans, and the more culturally hardline Conservative Party. Malcolm Jones a notable Conservative activist and Twitch streamer argued that the two parties would stand to become the most dominant forces in American politics if the then two year old alliance was ended. According to some, comeptition between the Conservative and Republican parties could lead to the two parties becoming the new two party system, replacing the Democratic Party entirely.

      2024 Elections

      The 2024 elections in the United States saw the alliance take control over the House of Representatives for the first time since 2014. The alliance would win a total of 258 out of the 435 total seats in the House, giving them a nearly 60% majority. They would also retake the Senate for the first time since the 2012 elections, winning 54 seats out of 100. The Congressional alliance members would then choose Charles Smith, who had just been re-elected to his second term in the Senate as the Majority Leader. In the House, the alliance chose Representative Michelle Dulles as Speaker of the House. Dulles had represented California's 17th Congressional District since 1978, and was a long-time ally of both the Villarreal and the McHale families. Thus many saw this as a way to bridge the divide between the two major parties of the alliance. In her first press interview as Speaker, Dulles vowed to reverse many of the policies of the Juarez administration. This included removing regulations for the beer industry, banks, and other financial institutions, as well as reversing the landmark 2022 Maxwell v. Simons Supreme Court Case that paved the way for increased access to abortions. Dulles went on to say that their win was a sign of a mandate from the people in favor of more fiscally and socially conservative positions in the face of "increasingly radical" Democratic politices. The alliance were able to secure a fairly narrow majority in the Senate with only 54 seats. Many in the media saw the crushing defeat for the American left as the end of the Juarez era in the United States.

      2025–2044: The Golden Age

      2025 began a period in which the Conservative–Republican Party was the dominant political party in national politics. The alliance held control over the Senate from 2025 to 2041, and held that House from 2025 to 2031, and from 2041 to 2049. During the period, the alliance held majorities in both the House and Senate after the 2026, 2028, 2030, 2040-2048 Congressional elections. During the end of the Juarez administration, the alliance were able to successfully block many of the remaining major policy proposals set out by Juarez in the 2020 election. Once Juarez was replaced with Jacob Villarreal, Congressional conservatives quickly moved to repeal the Right to Serve Act which prevented discrimination against members of the military who identified as LGBTQ+. The alliance would also repeal the Environmental Safety and Economic Prosperity Act thus paving the way for new fracking projects to begin. On March 2nd, Congress passed the Personal Responsibility and Work Requirement Act and the Welfare Fraud Prevention Act which caused a dramatic shift in the welfare scheme in the United States. After the two laws had passed, the responsibility to provide welfare to citizens was shifted from the federal government to state and local governments. Additionally, the two laws included new schemes to protect the system from claims of fraud and abuse. This was done by introducing means tests in order to determine eligibility for welfare recepients. The law would also de-register everyone from previous welfare programs in order to prevent those already committing fraud from being able to abuse the new system. These measures were quickly denounced by many including Democrats, the American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU) and many in the social work profession. According to these groups, the new system was unfair and disproportionally harsh to the vast majority of recepients who had not committed fraud. In late 2026 the American Health Care Act was passed. This version of the original American Health Care Act dramatically decreased eligibility for medicare and medicaid, and reduced the average benefits for medicare from $4,200 to $2,300 per year. The law also overrided the Americans with Mental Illnesses Act by re-allowing for insurance companies to charge varying rates for mental illnesses. The law also made it illegal for doctors to perform abortions and for a woman to receieve after 20 weeks of pregnancy, which would result in a maximum of 5 years in prision.

      The 2026 midterms saw a slight decrease in the alliance's majority in the House, with a gain of 2 seats in the Senate. Those two seats coming from the key battleground states of Pennsylvania and Michigan. The member parties would also sign the Joint Co-Operation and Election Competition Agreement in 2027, re-affirming the alliance's core values and pledging to avoid splintering the right-wing vote in all competitive districts. The alliance would also see the addition of the libertarian Social Co-operation Party, marking the first time a libertarian party joined the alliance. The alliance lost 8 seats in the House and gained 2 seats in the Senate while also maintaining their majority in both chambers. The alliance would also lose seats in the territorial legislatures of Guam and the Northern Mariana Islands. Losing their majority in the Northern Mariana Islands for the first time since 2016.

      The 2028 elections were won by the alliance with them retaining their majority in both chambers of Congress. The House would see an increase of 19 seats to the alliance due to fragmentation on the left. These included Michigan's 4th Congressional District, Maryland's 6th Congressional District and California's 16th Congressional District, all of which had held by Democrats since 2006. Their majority in the Senate however would decrease by 3 seats. The alliance would also retain all previously Republican held governorships. They would also win all the seats in the legislature of the Northern Mariana Islands for the first time ever.

      2045–2055

      2052 House of Delegates Elections

      Main article: 2052 United States House of Delegates Election

      In May of 2051, Democratic Senator Margaret Walsh introduced a bill that would create a third chamber of Congress that would be responsible for electoral borders and checking the powers of the President. This bill was propsed as a reaction to the removal of term limits passed in the 28th amendment. The bill received overhwelming support from Conservatives, as well as from centrist and moderate Democratic Representatives. Some who endorsed the bill included: Justin Finch, David Ortiz, and Blake Alexander. The bill would end up passing through the Senate with 3 Democrats and 1 Labour Senator breaking away from their party and voting for the bill. The bill would then pass through the House with an additional 13 Representatives from the Democratic and Labour parties voting in favor. The bill would then be vetoed by the Roderick Kendall, however Congress would override the veto, passing a new bill that created the House of Delegates. The 2052 midterm elections were chosen to be the first election for the newly created chamber.

      The alliance parties' main policy in the election was keeping President Kendall accountable for believed executive overreach. The campaign relied on keeping the executive branch of wielding too much authority over the daily lives of American citizens. Opposition parties ran on a platform of transparency and accountablility for lobbyists and other political donors. Going into the mid-terms the alliance parties held a slightly more lead in polls. Many pundits expected that the newly formed chamber would see an alliance majority, who could bring up impeachment charges against President Kendall. This would cause the left-wing voters to coalesce around keeping the alliance from turning this newly formed body into that of another partisan legislature. In many strong alliance states, no Democratic candidates was fielded, with a large focus on key states and territories. The U.S. territories of American Samoa, Guam, the Northern Mariana Islands, and the U.S. Virgin Islands were included in federal elections for the first time. For the alliance all of these territories were key battlegrounds in order to win a majority in the House of Delegates. Included in this campaigning were visits from Roderick Kendall to Guam and the Northern Mariana Islands.

      In the first ever elections for the House of Delegates, the CON–REP alliance won the vast majority of seats in the chamber with 66. No single party was able to win an outright majority in the House of Delegates. Therefore, the parties in the CON–REP alliance formed a coalition government with the far-right American Freedom Party. In addition, none of the alliance parties were the largest party, with the Labour Party being the largest party with 20 delegates. The largest alliance party was the Conservative Party who won 16 seats. The Liberal Party was able to upset the Republicans winning 15 seats and becoming the second largest party in the alliance. Republicans were the third largest party in the alliance with 14 seats. The centrist Alliance Party won 13 seats and the American Freedom Party won eight seats. Paulina DeLeon was then nominated as the President of the House of Delegates, and Walter Cranston was appointed as the first Speaker of the House of Delegates. Both of these two delegates would then quickly step down for their official party memberships and would take office as non-partisan presiding officers of the newly formed chamber. Surprising no one, both delegates from the Northern Mariana Islands were from the alliance, which was the largest non-Caucasian majority state or territory to send both elected officials from a right-wing party.

      Esther Clausen was elected as the first Majority Leader for the alliance, and Sarah Pearson became the first ever Majority Whip. Both positions are the highest partisan leadership positions in the chamber. Many saw the creation of this new third legislature and the large victory by the CON–REP alliance as a rebuke of the left-wing politics of the Kendall administration and perceived government over-reach. The main campaign platform of introducing articles of impeachment never manifested due to a lack of support from left-wing Delegates and from some Republicans. Former Vice President Mustafa Salaam, now Delegate from Maryland was one of the most vocal critics against this proposal, arguing that it was baseless and politically motivated. Articles of impeachment were introduced seven times in the House of Delegates and never passed the committee stage. Efforts were also made by alliance delegates to redistrict electoral boundaries in a favorable manner for the right; however, this too would fail to manifest.

      The newly formed chamber did successfully vote on the impeachment of U.S. Senator fom Kentucky Roy Cain amid accusations of sexual assault and rape by 10 former staffers from 1993 to 2050. He would subsequently be barred from holding any national public office for the rest of his life after the House found him guilty of sexually assaulting 4 out of the 10 former staffers and an additional 5 former campaign staffers.

      Logo of the Conservative party

      The primary official logo of the alliance consists of two circles and includes a bald eagle on a blue background with a circle of stars around the edges of the inside circle. The outer circle which is also the border of the logo is color in the primary official color of the alliance (#DE2E1B).       Factions within the Conservative Party

      National Conservatism

      National Conservatives are largest faction within the Conservative Party. This faction of the party are more involved with the culture wars, and preserving what is believed to be the cultural hertiage of the United States. As such, this faction takes a hard-line on immigration, and multiculturalism. Typically members of this faction are primarily focused on upholding traditional family values, and are strongly opposed to welfare, abortion, LGBT rights, and are much more strongly in favor of policies that promote law and order.

      This faction is represented by organizations such as the American Heritage Coalition and the Center for American Traditions and Values. In Congress, this faction has 23 members, 6 in the Senate and 17 in the House of Representatives. In 2030, Adam Johnson founded the Congressional National Conservative Caucus. This faction has been the most influential in the Conservative Party since the alliance began in 2010. Currently, the National Conservative faction's ideological leader is Helena Elizondo, the current Chair of the party. Other notable figures from this faction of the party include former Senator from Arizona Harry Warfton, Senator from Kentucky Sally McPherson, and Representative Martha Broom from Illinois' 4th congressional district. Paleoconservatives most often fall into the Elizondoist faction of the party.       There are a handful of a ideological camps in which members and supporters of the Conservative–Republican Party can be classified into.       Mainstream Conservatism

      Mainstream Conservatism is the largest faction within the alliance. The ideology can be classified as centrist to centre-right on the political spectrum. The beliefs of this faction include: cutting taxes, preservation of the family, increasing government spending, protecting the 2nd amendment. The Villarreal family are the primary torchbearers for this faction, with the majority of them being of centrist and fiscally conservative positions. This faction is primarily represented in the Republican Party and is the primary ideologieso of the Alliance and Liberal parties, with significant minorities in the Conservative Party. Generally members of this faction are for interventions in the middle east, and were overwhelmingly in favor of intervention in Morocco. They are also against changing the definition of marriage from a union between a man and a woman. Mainstream Conservatives favor free-market responses to social issues, and dislike regulating buisinesses. Conservatives also are against the legalization of marijuana. Elizondism and Radical Pragamtic ideologies originated from Mainstream Conservative thought. Typically, this ideology is favored by members of the middle class and by those who have a college education.

      Helena Elizondo Thoguht

      Main article: Political Positions of Helena Elizondo

      Helena Elizondo Thought, also known as Elizondoism is a political ideology based on the views of Helena Elizondo, a former United Tennis Federation Executive and current U.S. Senator from Florida. Elizondo's ideology has become the primary ideology of the Conservative Party. It shares similarities with one-nation conservatism im the U.K. Elizondo Thoguht can be considered more conservative than the more mainstream ideological dogma, as well as a right-wing populist ideology. Those who subscribe to this ideology typically are in favor of policies that would reduce income inequality while rejecting the idea of any systemic racial inequalities in the United States. This ideology also espouses anti-interventionsim, opposing any further intervention into foreign wars. Socially, followers of this ideology are culturally conservative, with the vast majority being white Evangelical Christians. Many followers of these beliefs are drawn to Elizondo due to her credentials as an Evangelical herself. Social views of this ideology include: opposition to LGBT rights, opposition to abortion, favorable views on the death penalty, rejecting "politcal correctness," being against teaching about systemtic inequality and historical racism. Economically, Elizondoism is a more standard fiscally conservative ideology. Elizondoism favors reducing taxes, reductions in educational funding, balancing the budget, cutting welfare programs, and is generally in favor of free-market capitalistic solutions to social issues. Elizondoism is a relatively new ideology as she has only recently become influential in the party, though her views have quickly become popular with the majority of the alliance's voter base.

      Overall, Elizondoism is popular with rural voters. It is also the most popular ideology of those who are either: white, non-college eudcated, and those over the age of 55. Elizondo's ideology is controversial due to her views on immigration, and on other issues in which the alliance has tried to change to appeal to a broader base of the U.S. population.

      Radical Pragmatism

      Main article: Radical Pragmatism

      Radical Pragmatism is the main political ideology of the moderate-conservative wing of the alliance. The faction are currently represented by the ideology's founder Jacob Villarreal, who implemented many of the policies during his Presidency. Radical Pragmatism can be considered fiscally conservative and socially progressive. It has also been described by its proponets as a "compassionate conservatism." Villarreal was opposed to things such as increases in taxes, and was in favor of increasing military funding. He was in favor of more liberal policies such as the legalization of marijuana, and most notably was in favor of improving social welfare. During his Presidency, Villarreal reversed some of the welfare programs put in place by his father during his Presidency. Villarreal also supported the legalization of same-sex marriage; however he never proposed any legislation during his tenure as a U.S. Senator or during his two terms as President. Villarreal was also notable for his pro-statehood views for the District of Columbia and Puerto Rico, introducing legislation in the Senate for a referendum on statehood. Those who are the most similar to Villarreal's ideology are opposed to the Elizondo faction of the alliance, and have threatened to leave and found a new party althogether on multiple occasions. The primary support base for this ideology comes from suburban Republicans, as well as from the Alliance and Liberal parties. The vast majority in the Conservative Party are strongly opposed to Villarreal's ideology; however they have always made compromises in order to maintain unity on the right.

      Puerto Rican Statehood

      Main article(s): 2046 Puerto Rican status referendum and Campaigning in the 2046 Puerto Rican statehood referendum

      See also: Puerto Rican Statehood Movement

      The Conservative–Republican Party had gone back and forth on their positions regarding Puerto Rican statehood. In their 2016 platform the alliance spoke in favor of statehood for the then territory of Puerto Rico. However during in the 2032, 2036, 2040, and 2044 platforms the alliance spoke of the status quo, without out-right saying that their position had changed on statehood. The majority of the voter base were against the ascension of Puerto Rico to a state. One poll put the percentage of Conservative Party voters in favor of statehood at just above 11%. Under the Jacob Villarreal's administration he signed into law the Puerto Rican Home Rule Act, which granted additional autonomy to Puerto Rico, and allowed Puerto Rico the right to vote in presidential elections. The law also relaxed some of the provisions of the Jones Act in an attempt to make the economy of Puerto Rico more self-sufficient. This was done in a effort to appease the growing sentiment in Puerto Rico for statehood. However, the opposite was true as Puerto Ricans found the new changes to be far from what they wanteed, which was statehood. In the Republican Party, the percentage of voters in favor of statehood was 36% according to a 2034 poll. Many notable Puerto Rican and hispanic members of Congress however spoke in favor of statehood. David Delgado, a U.S. Representative from Florida's 7th congressional district was a strong advocate for statehood. Along with him, 6 other Representatives and 3 Senators joined the Coalición del Movimiento Ciudadino (CCM), translated into english as the Citizen's Movement Coalition. Along with them left-wing representatives such as Joe Salvinia, Marcus Davenport, Cecilia Ramirez, and Andi Gomez all joined the caucus.

      Jacob Villarreal was a strong opponent of statehood because he believed that it would open the door to other territories and D.C. being granted statehood. Others opposed statehood for similar reasons. Some believed though that the opposition would be to adding another left-wing state to the union, rather than any genuine criticisms of potential statehood. Opposition to statehood was also common because of the belief that the island was fine in its current state. According to this arguement, the movement for statehood only comes around when the left is out of power.

      The caucus was able to gather enough support in 2045 to call for a referendum in Puerto Rico. Some members of the caucus began the Movement of Soverignty, which was a single-issue party advocating the a referendum on Puerto Rican statehood. The first referendum was whether or not Puerto Ricans wanted to either become a state or remain a territory. If the first referendum resulted in over 50% of Puerto Ricans in favor of statehood, a second referendum would be held in the United States on if Puerto Rico would be admitted into the union. The first referendum was held on November 5th, 2046 along with the 2046 midterm elections. The referendum campaign was bitter, and both sides spent millions of dollars in ads in order to swing the votes to their sides. Polls had the pro-statehood side narrowly ahead with 53% in favor and 43% opposed. The Conservative–Republican Party was split during the first referendum. Half of the party was campaigning for the pro-status quo campaign, and the other were campaign for Puerto Rican statehood. The pro-statehood Conservatives were labeled as "pretendientes," short for "los conservados pretendientes (pretender conservatives), calling into question their loyalty and conservative values. These efforts did not work as Puerto Ricans voted overwhelmingly in favor of statehood at 74% in the first round of the referendum.

      Presidential Elections

      Main article: List of Conservative-Republican presidential tickets

      The Conservative–Republican Party has stood for 12 Presidential elections since 2012. They have won only 3 and lost and 9; with the alliance losing 7 straight general elections to Roderick Kendall since 2032. The alliance has fielded 10 different candidates for President and 11 candidates for Vice President. 5 of the Vice Presidential candidates have been from the Republican Party, 5 have been from the Conservative Party and one was from the Liberal Party. All but one Presidential candidate fielded by the alliance has been from either the Conservative or Republican parties, with the only candidate not from either party was Sean Alexander from the Alliance Party. Four Presidential candidates were from the Conservative Party: Sean McHale, Jacob Villarreal, Ethan Soo and Helena Elizondo; with Villarreal being the only one to hold office.

      The largest defeat for the alliance was in the 2040 election, when they won only 222 electoral votes out of 538, and only 37.7% of the popular vote. The greatest victory for the alliance was in the 2012 election, when they won 301 electoral votes out of 538, and only 54.26% of the popular vote. The alliance's greatest popular vote victory was in 2028 when they won 65,053,294 / 119,750,297 (54.32%).
