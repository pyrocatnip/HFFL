Roderick Kendall (Born: July 11, 1989) is an American politican who is serving as the 45th President of the United States. He served as the Senate Minority Leader from 2029–2031, and as the Chair of the Senate Democratic Caucus from 2027–2031. Prior to his term as as U.S. Senator, Kendall served as the 33rd Governor of Oregon from 2015 until 2027. He is joined the Labour Party. He was previously a member of the Democratic Party until 2032, and was an independent in 2033.

Kendall was born in Elizabethtown, Oregon. He would attend the University of Elizabethtown, where he would obtain a Bachelors of Arts. He would then finish his studies at the University, earning a law degree. He would enter political office in 2015 winning the race for Governor of Oregon. At the time of his victory, Kendall became the youngest person to ever be elected to the position. He would serve three terms as Governor, winning re-election in 2019, and 2022. Kendall would then choose not to run for re-election in 2026, instead opting to challenger long-time Democratic incumbent Rosalia Driver for the United State Senate. He won the primary, and placing first with 38.7% of the primary vote to Driver's 32.7%. He would then unseat Driver in a narrow general election, winning 50.2% to 49.8%. Kendall would only spend one term in the U.S. Senate, however in that term he was elected as the Chair of the Senate Democratic Caucus, becoming the first freshman Senator to win the position. Then following the 2031 election, Kendall won the race for the Senate Minority Leader. Kendall would decide to run for President, rather than running for re-election.

He would then serve seven terms as President of the United States from 2033 until 2061. He would be the first President to serve more than two terms since Franklin D. Roosevelt. Kendall announced during his acceptance speech for the Labour Party Presidential candidate that he would not run for re-election in 2060.

Early Life (1989–2007)

Kendall's mother Agatha Klein was born in 1935 in Berlin to a poor Jewish family. Agatha would flee to the United States in 1934 from Nazi Germany. She moved to Brooklyn as a child and grew up in Brooklyn, New York. Agatha would get a job Bell as a telephone operator. She would meet Kendall's father Joesph Kendall in 1954, and got married in 1940. Joesph would and Agatha would have their first child, a daughter who they named Isabella. They would have their second child, Roderick, in 1960. Isabella would attend the University of Oregon and graduate in 1966, she would earn a Bachelors of Science in Microbiology and would attend the University of Southern California, where she would attend medical school and obtain a M.D. as a Pediatrician. She would work in a hospital from 1976 until 1988, then she would work in a private practice until she retired in 2010. His father, Joesph would attend Princeton University and obtained a masters in Psychology. He would work as a therapist before retiring in 2007.

Roderick Kendall was born on July 11, 1989 in Elizabethtown, Oregon. He attended St. John's School, a private Catholic school from 5 years old until 5th grade. He would then attend Elizabethtown Middle School from 6th grade to 8th grade. Kendall attended Lincoln High School. In high school, he was a member of the debate team, and joined the Young Democrats of Oregon. Kendall graduated in 1970, and attended the University of Southern California Law School with a Doctorate in Law. He would grow up in Elizabethtown, and attended Elizabethtown High School. He would then attend the University of Elizabethtown, where he would obtain his Bachelors degree. He would then transfer to John's Hopkins University, where he would obtain a law degree.

Professional Career (2007–20010)

After graduating from college, Kendall moved to Portland, Oregon. There he would work at a law firm for the first two years after his graduation. However, he would quit his job in 20004 and would spend time as a community organizer from 2004 until 2010. During this time, he became more active in politics, and even spent time working for the Adam Jones' Gubernatorial re-election campaign. During this time, Kendall's interest in running for office grew. He would also work as a public defender for the state of Oregon from 2010 until 2015.

Kendall would also advocate for the expulsion of long-time ally, U.S. Senator for Oregon Roy Cain due to accusations of corruption, destruction of evidence, and sexual harrassment of a staffer. He also wrote an op-ed in the Elizabethtown Daily, calling for the Democratic Party to expell Cain. Once it became clear that incumbent Governor Adam Jones was not going to re-call Senator Cain, Kendall would join in on protests in the capital, and would write a second op-ed demanding that Governor Jones re-calls Adam Jones. Cain would win re-election against Republican Sarah Chavez in the 2013 U.S. Senate election.

Kendall would marry his longtime girlfriend Abigail Navarro on April 23rd, 2010.

Governor of Oregon (2015–2023)

Elections

Main article: 2014 Oregon gubernatorial election, 2018 Oregon gubernatorial election, and 2022 Oregon gubernatorial election

Kendall ran for governor in 2014. In the primary, he would defeat long-time incumbent Oregon Governor Adam Jones. Kendall would choose lawyer Braden McMahon as his Lieutenant Governor. He would then win in the general election with a majority of the vote to 54.8% to Republican challenger Sarah Glover's 46.2%. He would campaign on a progressive platform, including raising the minimum wage, increases in funding to social programs, and increasing taxes on corporations. Kendall's platform appealed to a younger and more progressive population, with additional gained support from working class voters who would have normally voted Republican. Kendall was also able to leverage social media in order to increase turnout to the general election.

Kendall would win re-election in 2018, defeating Republican challenger Shaun Pollock in a landslide victory. He would then win a third term as Governor, this time defeating Victoria Islas. Kendall would win the election and increased his margin of victory from 74.2% of the vote in 2018 to 80.6% in 2022.

    First Team (2015–2019)

    In his first 100 days as Governor; Kendall would sign major legislation increasing taxes on corporations, as well as a law that increased the state's minimum wage to $15/hour. On January 28, 2015, Kendall signed the Oregon Voting Rights Act of 2015, which would increase voter rights for people of color by faciliating more voter registration programs, eliminating voter ID restrictions, and allowing mail-in and same-day voting. The also made any act to prevent citizens of Oregon from voting in any election illegal. On February 1, 2015, Kendall signed into law the Educational Rights Act of 2015. This law made preschool and kindergarden free for all Oregonians, and would establish the foundation for Universal Community College in the state of Oregon. The bill made it through the legislature of Oregon with a majority due to the overwhelimg Democratic majority in both chambers of the legislature. The following week, he would sign into law the Transparency in Government Act, requiring all campaign donations to be publicaly availble, as well as requiring all campaign meetings and all campaign finances to be publicly available through a state-run website. Once he was elected, Kendall would re-call Roy Cain from the U.S. Senate and would appoint Victoria Deleon in his place. The following year, a special election was held in which Deleon was able to secure her seat in the Senate.

    His second year in office saw Kendall making minor reforms to the state's finances. This included a budget that increased corporate tax rates to 38%, and closed many loopholes preventing corporations from being able to avoid paying taxes. Kendall would also sign an Executive Order requiring an investigation of 21 5011(c) corporations that were accused of incorrect or missing tax returns. Kendall would however come under fire, following the revelation that his 2016-17 budget included a 11.5% increase in state police funds, as well as efforts to increase police training and police militarization.

    Second Term (2019–2023)

    Kendall was able to easily secure a second term as Governor in 2018, having no primary challenger, and then defeating Republican Shaun Pollock in the general election. On March 18th, 2018, Kendall signed into law the Oregon Educational Reform Act of 2018. This law amended the Educational Rights Act of 2015, with the primary change to the law being the creation of a Universal Community College in the state of Oregon, and requiring unbiased and comprehensive sex education for all high schoolers.

    Abortion

    Early into his third terms a Governor, Kendall signed the Women's Health Act legalizing all abortions. Included in the law were additional funds for comprehensive sex education and funds to ensure that birth control pills were accessible to the citizens of Oregon.

    Capital Punishment and Criminal Legal Reform

    Kendall signed the Criminal Justice Reform Act in 2020, therefore outlawing the use of the death penalty in the state of Oregon. He would also commute the sentences of 15 non-violent offenders.

    U.S. Senate (2023–2033)

    First Term (2023–2027)

    Roderick Kendall was sworn in as a senator on January 3, 2027, he then joined the Congressional Progressive Caucus. He would vote in favor of the repal of the controversial Don't Ask Don't Tell policy encted by Trent Villarreal. He would also co-sponsor a bill with Villarreal's son, and Kendall immediate predecessor as President, Jacob Villarreal, and his future Vice President Mustafa Salaam on the Dignity in Housing Act of 2024. Kendall also voted for the Women's Healthcare Act of 2024, which would've codified the ruling of Roe v. Wade into law. In 2025, Kendall introduced the Public Records Act, which would have required that all meetings of government officials have a publicly accessible record. In May of 2026 Kendall introduced the Recreational Marijuana Legalization Act. Kendall would vote against the Corproate Accountability Act of 2027. He would also co-sponsor the failed Voting Rights Act of 2027.

    Second Term (2027–2033)

    Kendall won re-election for a second term in the U.S. Senate. During his second term, Kendall would take over as the Senate Minority Leader following the retirement of Senator Herbet Wu (D–CA). During the campaign, Kendall campaigned across the country to get out votes for Democratic Senate candidates in order to regain a Senate majority, which Democrats had failed to achieve since 2010. On February 10, 2027, Kendall introduced the D.C. Representation Act of 2027, which would have increased home rule for the District; however the bill failed to make it through committee. On March 11, he introduced the Capital Gains Taxation Act, which also failed to make it through the Republican-Conservative party controlled appropriations committee.

    Chair of the Senate Democratic Caucus

    Soon after taking office for his first term as a Senator, Kendall would nominate himself for the election for the Senate Minority Leader. Kendall would lose this election to Stephen Wilson. Kendall would then run for the Chair of the Senate Democratic Caucus, and won the position in a landslide.

    Murder of La'Sean Johnson

    Main articles: Murder of La'Sean Johnson, 2933-34 United States racial unrest and Protests in Dougalass regarding the trial of Carl Williams

    La'Sean Johnson was a 21-year-old college student at Georgetown University, located in Washington D.C. At 12:51 PM EST, Johnson was walking to his off-campus dorm in the neighborhood, when he was stopped by then officer Carl Williams. Williams would initially detain Johnson due to allegedly matching the profile of a suspect who shoplifted from multiple stores in the area. At 12:15, Johnson was handcuffed by Officer Williams and was eventually shot nine times in the head and neck, immediately killing Johnson. The whole incident was recorded on the Officer's body camera, and was also being recorded by bystanders. Johnson would be immediately placed on administrative leave the day, upon the release of the video to social media mere hours after the incident occured. Williams would then be investigated due to inconsistencies in his offical police report.

    Kendall was quick to denounce the murder of La'Sean Johnson, and called for a full un-biased investigation into the incident in a press statement released on the social media platform Twitter. However, Kendall would stop short of calling the incident a case of police brutality. On February 10th, 2033, Kendall met with the family of La'Seasn Johnson in the White House. He would then speak on national television, discussing the event, and once again calling for a full investigation. Kendall would also call on the public to "gather peacfully," in protests and to avoid acts of violence or looting. Unfortunately, this did not prevent such actions from taking place, as riots and looting were occuring across the country, along with peaceful protests and violent riots. Kendall also stated that the media failed to correctly cover the protests that were taking place. He also called for unity and asked that the country wait for the full facts of the case to emerge before making a judgement.

    This official statement was met with criticsm due to the fact that video clearly showed that officer Williams had used excessive force during his detainment of Johnson. Kendall would then release a public apology in the press, stating that he apologized to the family of Johnson for the loss of their son, and to the public for his poor word choice.

    The following week, as protests began to increase, Kendall would deploy the National Guard into D.C. Once again, his actions were met with backlash, calling the use of the National Guard an "occupying army in the nation's capital." Kendall would then enforce a curfeww in D.C., prohibting people from being outside of their houses after 10PM. This would last in the District until the end of the trial, which didn't end until May of the following year.

    Second Term (2037–2041)

    As a result of his actions following the protests that occurred in the District of Columbia following the death of La'Sean Johnson, Knedall would lose in the Democratic primaries and general election for the District in the 2036 election. This would mark the first time since 1984 that a Democratic incumbent would lose in D.C. primaries. Instead, the 5 delegates for the District were given to the D.C. Statehood Party.

    Seventh Term (2057–Present)

    Main articles: 2056 United States Presidential Election, Presidency of Roderick Kendall

    Kendall was re-elected to his seventh and final consecutive term as President of the United States on November 7th, 2056. Kendall won 279 electoral college votes to Conservative–Republican challenger Robert Jackson. Kendall's term began on January 20, 2057 and will end on January 20, 2061. Kendall announced in his acceptance speech for the 2056 Labour Party National Convention that whether he won or lost, 2056 would be his final time as a candidate in a Presidential election. He said that it was time for new leadership to take over, and for a new direction in the Labour Party more generally. Kendall would cite wanting to spend time with family and the stresses of the job as his main personal reasons for deciding to retire from public service.

    Political Positions

    Main article: Political Positions of Roderick Kendall

    Poltical Party and Organization Affiliations

    Kendall registered as a Democratic at the age of 15, and would eventually become a full member of the party in 2012. He would join multiple Democratic youth organizations during college, and would spend time working in multiple campaigns during his high school and college years. Kendall joined the Oregon chapter of the Vote Blue campaign, an organization dedicated to registering Democratic voters, and encourage them to participate in elections. During his tenure as Governor of Oregon, Kendall would advocate for the Democratic Party to adopt more progressive value in order to return to the original roots of the party. By the summer of 2032, following his nomination for President, Kendall would become dissolusioned with the Democratic establishment and a year in his first term as President, he would become and independent.

    In his letter A Message to the Democratic Party, Kendall would criticize the party's senior officials for being too open to "generous donations from the interests of corporations." He would write that he was proud to work for the party that stood for the little guy, however in his eyes, that was no longer true of the party. Only a month after declaring himself to be an independent, Kendall joined the Labour Party, a progressive left-wing social democratic party.
