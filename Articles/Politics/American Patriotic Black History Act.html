Policies

The American Patriotic Black History Act (APBHA) is a United States Federal law signed by President Helena Elizondo on October 13, 2049. The act established new standard educational curricula for American public education that would mandate "fair and impartial education" in all primary and secondary educational curricula. The act was called an act of censorhip in schools by many human rights activists, students, and some parents. The bill passed through the Republican-controlled Congress, and passed along party lines, with all but one Democrat supporting the bill in the House. The law receieved support from many Conservatives, who championed anti-woke education, and "school-choice." The bill was a major piece of legislation durng the Parents' Revolution during the 2030s and 40s.

The bill was amended two times, the first amendment to the bill changed what is allowed to be taught in sex education. The bill prohibited discussing birth control and abortions in all federally funded programs, as well as removed all mentions of Planned Parenthood. The second amendement made to the bill repealed the most controvsial aspect of the bill, which was the Anti-Critical Race Theory hotlines. The act remains controversial to this day, and Democratic lawmakers have tried on numerous occasions to repeal the law, with all attempts thus far failing.

Background

The APBHA was propsed amidst the backdrop of the Parents' Revolution movement. Similar bills had been proposed in the 119th and 120th Congresses of the United States, however they failed to make it through Congress due to Democrats holding a majority in one or both chambers. Once the 121st Congress assembled for the first time, it was expected that members would propose a bill similar to the ones proposed in the past. State bills had also been passed in Alabama, Kentucky, Texas and Virginia, and support for a "Patriotic education," was high among Republicans and was fairly high with independent voters. The state laws were challenged in court, however each of state's supreme courts upheld the rulings in each case.

Legislative History

The APHBHA was proposed by Republican Congressman Aaron Jones on September 11, 2049. The bill was assigned to the House Educcation and Labor Committee on September 12th. The bill would be approved by the committee on the 18th of September, at which point it was voted on by the chamber-at-large. The bill passed in the House due to the majority control being held by the Repubnlicans who widely supported the bill. No Democrats in the House voted for the bill, though they were in the minority; therefore the bill passed the House on party lines. Democrats attempted to fillabuster the bill, however they didn't gain enough support from Republicans to trigger a fillabuster. The final vote in the House was 301 for and 237 opposed. The bill would enter the Senate on September 28th, and would be voted on the very next day. The vote in Senate went mostly down party lines with all of the 56 Republican Senators voting in favor along with 1 Democrat, 42 Democrats voted against, and two Democrats abstained. The bill would then move on to the joint conference committee on October 3rd. The House voted again, this time 302 in favor with 230 opposed, and 6 abstensions. The Senate voted 57 42, therefore passing Congress on October 7th.

Provisions of the Act

Title I – Fairness in Education

  • Amends the No Child Left Behind Act to allow for parents to have more options for schooling
  • Prohibits discrimination in school choice based on race
  • Establishes independent research to determine the success of school choice

Title II – Accurate Curricula

  • Prevents the term "critical race theory" from being taught in K-12 schools
  • Removes / prohibits mentions of systemtic racism from appearing in school textbooks and curricula
  • Empowers school systems to create banned book lists, which would prevent the books from appearing in school libraries
  • Requires schools to remove all mention of non-heterosexual sexual orientations
  • Requires schools to remove all mention of non-cisgendered / male-female gender binaries
  • Mandates "balanced" views in all history textbooks
  • Removes all material intended to guilt Americans for the past

Title IIII – Critical Race Theory Hotline

  • Establishes a national hotline to report any mentions of Critical Race Theory in textbooks or classrooms
  • Establishes fines for discussions of systemtic racism

School Choice Controversy

Controversy Regarding the Tuskegee Syphilis Experiments

Parents in the Los Angeles County Public Schools complained to the school board, that the textbooks provided under the APBHA removed all mentions of the Tusgkegee Syphilis Study that took place from 1932 unti 1972. Particularly, African-American families raised concerns regarding the whitewashing of this part of U.S. history, believing that it was ignored in order to further a specific narrative of American exeptionalism. The families of 10 students sued the school board for imporperly educating their child on February 10, 2058. The case was brought in front of the Los Angeles County Superior Court, who ruled that the school system had in-fact failed their duty in educating students by erasing mentions of Tuskegee, and of the history of lynch mobs from school textbooks. The ruling was appealed and reached the California Supreme Court, who overturned the ruling of the Superior Court, stating that the school boards were following federal guidelines set by the APBHA.

The issue of removing historical events would come into the spotlight again when on May 11th, Native-American rights groups sued the school board of Los Angeles for the removal of any mentions of the trail of tears in textbooks. The court ruled was in favor of the school board yet again.

Attempts to the Repeal the Law

  • June 18, 2051 – Sean Paul (D-CA-49th) introduced H.R. 8319, which would've repealed the bill in its entirety, the bill was failed to make it past the House being rejected 310-201.
  • July 1, 2051 – Jason Gay (D-VA-30th) introduced H.R. 8422 calling for an end to anti-CRT hotlines in the United States. The bill failed to make it past the education committee.
  • August 20, 2051 – Sen.April Lu (D-NY) introduced S.B. 6046 calling for an end to anti-CRT hotlines in the United States. The bill failed to make it past the education committee.
  • August 23, 2051 – Jason Gay (D-VA-30th) introduced H.Res. 722 to have Congress officially denounce the bill as a violation of civil rights.
  • December 8, 2051 – Lance Young (D-TX-1st) introduced H.R. 9918 to repeal the currciula standards outlined in Section II. The bill would make it through the House, however, the Senate rejected the Bill, 52-44.
